Tokenised LLM · Lesson 01 · Feasibility

The hard part isn't the tokens

Why attribution, not payment, decides whether a tokenised LLM is buildable — and the one diagram that lets you judge any pitch on sight.

Your mission is a verdict: is a tokenised LLM — one that pays people when someone else's query uses their knowledge — actually worth building? To answer that you need to know where the difficulty really lives. This lesson plants the flag.

Every tokenised LLM is two layers

Pull any such system apart and you find the same two layers:

LayerQuestion it answersHow hard
AttributionWhose contribution was responsible for this answer, and how much?Very hard
SettlementHow do we pay them for it?Mostly solved

The token machinery — wallets, smart contracts, splitting a payment ten ways — is ordinary plumbing. It works today. So the feasibility of the whole idea collapses onto one question: can you honestly say who contributed to an answer? That depends entirely on how you build the model.

The one mental model

The Bake ↔ Retrieve spectrum. The more a contributor's knowledge is baked into the model's weights, the harder it is to attribute. The more it stays in retrievable, discrete units, the easier. Where a system sits on this line tells you how defensible its reward claim is.

Retrieveknowledge kept separate (RAG)
→ attribution is provable Bakeknowledge fused into weights
→ attribution is approximate

Why baking makes attribution hard

When you train (or fine-tune) one model on everyone's data, each contribution is smeared across billions of parameters. To recover "how much did Alice's document matter to this output?" you can't just look it up — you have to estimate it. The two serious tools for this both have a sting:

  • Influence functions (e.g. TRAK) estimate a training example's effect without retraining — but at LLM scale they stay approximate and assumption-laden; they assume the model perfectly minimised its training error, and real "fitting errors" corrupt the scores.12
  • Data Shapley gives a provably fair split borrowed from game theory — but exact computation requires retraining the model on every possible subset of the data. That's astronomically expensive; in practice you can only approximate it.3

Notice the bind: the method that's fair (Shapley) is intractable, and the method that's tractable (influence functions) is only approximate. Neither gives you a number you'd happily defend to a contributor who asks "why was I paid £3 and not £30?" — and that disputability is itself a feasibility risk.

Why retrieval makes it provable

Now flip to the other end. Keep each contribution as a discrete document in a store. The base model does the reasoning; the knowledge is retrieved at query time. When a query pulls in Alice's chunk and Bob's chunk to build the answer, you have a log: these chunks, this query, this much weight each. That log is auditable. You can pay against it without anyone having to trust a black-box estimate.

This is why, for a feasibility-minded build, the retrieve end is the pragmatic home base — not because it's fashionable, but because the attribution is real. The trade-off: you only attribute what was retrieved, not the base model's own latent reasoning. That's a scope you can live with; an undefendable payment isn't.

The win

You can now do the thing the mission asks: take any "tokenised / decentralised AI" pitch and immediately ask where does its knowledge live? If the honest answer is "baked into one model," its attribution — and therefore its whole fairness claim — rests on approximations. That's not fatal, but it's the question that separates real from hand-wavy.
