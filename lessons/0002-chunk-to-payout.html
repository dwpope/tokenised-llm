Tokenised LLM · Lesson 02 · Feasibility

From chunk to payout

Tracing the auditable log end to end — and the one gap that decides whether the whole thing is honest.

Lesson 01 ended on a promise: at the retrieve end of the spectrum, you get an auditable log you can pay against. This lesson cashes that promise — literally — and then shows you the crack in it that every serious builder has to confront.1

The pipeline, end to end

A retrieval-based tokenised LLM is a six-step assembly line. Money is made at the end and split backwards.

  1. 1Contribute. A contributor adds a document. It's split into chunks, each turned into an embedding (a vector) and stored with an owner ID.
  2. 2Ask. A user submits a query. It's embedded into the same vector space.
  3. 3Retrieve. The store returns the top-k nearest chunks, each with a similarity score.
  4. 4Generate. The base model writes an answer using those chunks as context.
  5. 5Meter. Log which chunks were in play, and a weight for each (e.g. its similarity share).
  6. 6Settle. Split the query's revenue across the chunk owners, in proportion to those weights.
The shape of it

Notice steps 1–4 are just ordinary RAG — thousands of products do this. The tokenisation is only steps 5 and 6: keep an owner ID on every chunk, and write a usage log you can pay against. That's why the reward layer is "the easy part."

The crack: retrieved ≠ used

Here is the gap that decides everything. Step 5 assumes that a retrieved chunk was actually used to write the answer. Often it wasn't. The base model frequently answers from its own baked-in knowledge and then attaches a citation to the nearest retrieved chunk — a behaviour researchers call post-rationalisation.2

The numbers are sobering. In long-form answers, 50–90% of citations aren't fully supported by the source they point to, and citation-hallucination rates run 11–57% across deployed models.2 If you pay by "what was cited," you are paying for a story the model told about its own reasoning — not for the knowledge it truly used.

This is the feasibility crux of the whole idea. It doesn't kill a tokenised LLM — but it means a credible system needs more than a retrieval log. It needs a faithfulness check: evidence that the answer actually depends on the chunk (e.g. does the answer change if you remove it?). That check is the real engineering, and the real cost.

So weighting is a policy, and policies get gamed

Because "how much did this chunk contribute" is never handed to you cleanly, you must choose a rule — and every rule has an attack:

PolicyAttack it invites
Similarity-weightedFlood the store with near-duplicate chunks to capture retrieval.
Equal split among retrievedStuff many thin chunks so more of yours get pulled in.
Model-reported citationsRewards post-rationalised citations — pays for hallucinated credit.

The win

You can now draw the full money path — contribute → retrieve → generate → meter → settle — and, more importantly, you can name the exact place it gets dishonest: the leap from retrieved to actually used. When someone pitches "we fairly pay contributors via RAG," your question is now sharp: how do you verify the answer truly depended on the chunk you're paying for?
